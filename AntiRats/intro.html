Introduction

The aim of this project is to provide an overview of the many critics of Enlightenment rationalism. The essays on each thinker are intended not merely to offer an overview of that thinker, but also to place him or her in the context of this larger stream of anti-rationalist thought.

Some my wonder at the mixed bag of thinkers we address: poets, philosophers, economists, mathematicians, novelists, political theorists, urbanists, and philosophers. But there is unity in this diversity.

As I think I have shown in my work on Oakeshott, his political criticism of rationalism sprang directly from his philosophical view of the modes of experience: the rationalist in politics was guilty of an ignoratio elenchi. And what would a general critique would be but a philosophical one expressed without technical philosophical language. When Eliot writes of men "dreaming of systems so perfect that no one has to be good," he is expressing in poetry what Oakeshott expressed in verse. (And they share a common source in Bradley!)
