Martin Heidegger: The Limiting Character of the Scientific Method

Jack Simmons

Abstract

In the hands of Edmund Husserl and Martin Heidegger, phenomenology became a way of reflecting upon the rigor of philosophy and science. While Husserl aimed at adding rigor to philosophical investigation, Heidegger, more profoundly, reveals the inherent weaknesses of the sciences, such as mathematics, physics and biology, in “obtaining and securing the primary way of access to what are supposedly the objects of this science.” Heidegger claims that the phenomenological method reveals the a priori conditions necessary for the sciences mentioned above, and the inherent weaknesses and blind spots of those disciplines. In this chapter, I propose to explain Heidegger’s criticism of scientific reasoning along with his call for a more profound approach to thinking that references both medieval and pre-Socratic world-views.
