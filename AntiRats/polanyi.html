Michael Polanyi: A Scientist Against Scientism

Charles W. Lowney

Since the beginning of the Modern era, and into the Enlightenment, science and its method have been seen as the way to overcome the darkness and the distortion that religious authority and powerful interests could disseminate. It is still a common conception in the 21st century that science, in opposition to religions and traditions, provides, or can provide, all the answers that we need. Michael Polanyi was a scientist and philosopher who showed that the Enlightenment's standards for knowledge were distortions that could have destructive effects. It's critical rationalism led to a "scientism" in which meaning and human value were illusions and only facts based in physics could be affirmed as true. Alternatively, it also led to a post-modern repudiation of objectivity in which truth itself was an illusion, subservient to subjective emotion or political power. Polanyi's post-critical philosophy revised Enlightenment standards to more accurately reflect how science does proceed. He revised the understanding of (1) objective truth, (2) the separation of mind/value from world/fact, (3) a Cartesian explicit analysis to simplest self-evident truths, and (4) its concomitant reductive analysis of reality to smallest physical parts. He did this with his conceptions of (1) personal knowledge, (2) indwelling, (3) tacit knowing, and (4) emergent being. Polanyi showed that science actually moves forward toward truth and better contact with reality by using the same tools of practical knowing that can affirm the efforts of cultural and religious traditions that are open to dialogue and discovery. His way of looking at truth and reality allows that values, and not just physical facts, can be real discoveries about the world. He opens up possibilities for understanding beyond simple dualisms and reductionisms, forestalls a regression into nihilism, and renews hope in human progress.
